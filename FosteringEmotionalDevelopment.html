Introduction of Fostering Emotional Development

Emotional development is the emergence of the experience, expression, understanding, and regulation of emotions from birth and the growth and change in these capacities throughout childhood, adolescence, and adulthood. The development of emotions occurs in conjunction with neural, cognitive, and behavioral development and emerges within a particular social and cultural context (Izard and Trentacosta 2020, para. 1) Teaching Competencies and Skills . Children learn best in the context of warm, positive, respectful, caring and responsive relationships (Evans & Harvey, 2012). Teachers need to actively develop their relationships with young children and their families (Bary, 2010). . Creating a Positive Climate: Setting a positive climate helps children feel safe and secure in their environment. Climate refers to the prevailing mood, attitudes and tone that teachers, families and children feel when they are in the early childhood setting. When there is a positive climate and children feel good, they are more likely to interact with others. . Teachers can help children build confidence and develop a positive self-identity. It is important to note that confidence and positive self-identity are learnt skills which develop over time. . Show respect for children and build them up in a positive and authentic way. This might include letting children know about the things that you think makes them interesting and unique, asking children to talk about what they like about themselves, and modelling a positive self-identity by talking about what you like about yourself. Techniques for nurturing Emotional Development, Learning and Wellbeing Create environments where all students feel valued and supported by sta and peers. Students are then more likely to build on this feeling of belonging by connecting further with their school community. They are also more likely to experience increased feelings of competence, engagement, and intrinsic motivation to learn, pursue their interests, and thrive. Providing clear structure and routines, and access to safe spaces, are particularly important for students who experience mental health challenges, or who have experienced trauma or adverse childhood experiences (Trauma and Resilience Educator Project,2023) Secure, respectful, and reciprocal relationships lead to students’ increased engagement in learning, prosocial behavior, feelings of safety at school, and reduced depression, anxiety and stress. Authentic Curriculum plan and examples

Art
Children create self-portraits showing a specific feeling (e.g., joy, frustration) and share the story behind it. This activity encourages self-awareness as children can identify their feeling and reflect on what caused the emotion.
Drama and Puppetry:
Educator can use puppets to model emotional challenges. This activity helps children observe emotion like sadness, frustration, and learn to identify in other. This activity also encourages children to think cooperatively.
Movement and music
Encourage children to work on team or create group dance where they can make body movement following the rhythm where each body movement, gestures which represent di erent kind of emotion.

Learning experiences

1. For 0 to 12 months


Mirror plays
Activity: For this activity we can place mirror on the eye level of baby during tummy time or on the floor. As baby see on the mirror educator can make di erent faces looking in the mirror or copying the expression baby are making and talk about the expressions with baby that is seen in the mirror.
Outcome: This activity helps babies to identify themselves. By looking their own reflection and facial expression, they can learn to associate facial expressions with emotions. It helps to encourage and foster bonding with educators.

2. For 12 to 24 months

Emotion sorting game
Activity: For the activity we need some simple emotion cards like happy, sad and angry and some bins or containers with matching emotion colors. Educator can show card to toddler and ask them to put in the container like for example “this is happy face it should go in yellow bin”.
Outcome: This activity helps to develop a toddler deeper understanding of how emotion look like. As toddler are sorting and placing the cards on the bins it also helps to develop their fine motor skills. It also supports their language development as they name the emotion they see.

For 2 to 3 years
Musical emotion chair
Activity: For this activity we need to set up some chair labeled with emotion. After that we can play a music and once a music is stopped children need to sit down on the chair and express the emotion that are labeled in the chair they are sitting. Outcome: This activity allows children to express their emotion through body language and movement. It helps them to better understand about how emotion feel on their body and how they can express. The activity also fosters gross motor skills as children move around on the combination of music.
