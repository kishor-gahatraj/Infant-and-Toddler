Introduction to Perceptual Development

Infants and toddlers learn to understand sensory information they detect through eyesight together with hearing and touching and tasting and smelling. The sensory experiences enable children to develop self-awareness together with social awareness and environmental understanding (Sheridan et al., 2011). The early childhood stage depends exclusively on sensory perception to both discover and comprehend and answer to the world around them. A child's perceptual development requires secure spaces which contain sensory-stimulation opportunities for feeling observations through touch and seeing through eyes and hearing sounds and tasting substances and sensing scents (Berk & Meyers, 2019). Teachers are essential in observing child responses to select appropriate sensory learning experiences which lead to meaningful outcomes. Children's education through perceptual development leads to meaningful environments by supporting the principles of belonging and being and becoming (Department of Education, 2022; Roth et al., 2019) according to the Early Years Learning Framework (EYLF).

Teaching Competencies and Skills

Educators have a leading role in perceptual development through their work towards building sensory-stimulating learning spaces as well as their active engagement with children (Gartrell, 2020). For developing this competency successfully one must acquire specific abilities and important skills. The assessment of comfort levels and preferences can be achieved through monitoring sensory responses of children (Sheridan et al., 2011). The provision of appropriate sensory elements such as fabric together with water and sand and lighting and natural items supports multisensory learning (MacBlain, 2021).
You can boost language development together with cognitive skills by being perceptive toward sensory experiences of children and intentionally using verbalization ("You felt cold water") (Berk & Meyers, 2019).
Daily teaching approaches must provide an environment balanced for senses through dedicated quiet and active play areas to serve students with di erent needs (Curtis & Ohagan, 2019).
Daily nappy changes and snack times and outdoor play sessions serve as opportunities where performers can present di erent smells and sounds and textures that enhance learning within the daily caregiving schedule (Raising Children Network, 2023).
